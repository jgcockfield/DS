UNESCO World Heritage Site  ·  Louisiana  ·  1700 – 1100 BCE

Poverty Point

A monument built 3,400 years ago by hunter-gatherers — before the wheel reached the Americas, before metal tools, before agriculture took hold in this valley.

The Discovery

What LiDAR Changed

For decades, archaeologists working at Poverty Point could only see fragments — partial ridges, isolated mounds, features obscured by trees and time. The full scale of what had been built was not visible from the ground.

When LiDAR stripped away the vegetation, it revealed a precisely engineered complex stretching nearly a mile across — six concentric C-shaped ridges, a massive central plaza, and a bird-shaped mound larger than the Great Pyramid of Giza by volume.

Poverty Point challenges everything we thought we knew about what hunter-gatherers could build — and why they would choose to.

The questions that followed have only deepened. Who organized this? What drew thousands of people here? And why did it stop?

The Site

3,400 Years Ago in Northeastern Louisiana

The people who built Poverty Point left no written records. What we know comes from what they moved: over 53 million cubic feet of earth, carried in baskets by hand, shaped into a geometric precision that still registers from satellite altitude today.

They were not farmers. They were hunter-fisher-gatherers operating in one of the most resource-rich environments on the continent — the lower Mississippi Valley at peak post-glacial abundance. They traded across a network spanning from the Great Lakes to the Gulf Coast.

The UNESCO nomination file describes Poverty Point as outstanding universal value — not merely for its scale, but for what it proves: that social complexity, monumental ambition, and long-distance coordination are not the exclusive property of agricultural civilizations.

This is a conversation about what we know, what we have reasonably inferred, and what remains among the great unresolved questions in North American archaeology.

Plan View — Concentric Ridge System

Six concentric C-shaped ridges open eastward toward Bayou Macon. Mound A (bird effigy) stands to the northwest. The central plaza covers roughly 37 acres.

Cross Section — Mound & Ridge Heights

Mound A rises 22 metres — comparable in volume to the Great Pyramid of Giza. The six residential ridges stand 1.5 to 2 metres tall and up to 80 metres wide at their base.
